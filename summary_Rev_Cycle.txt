# Revenue Cycle Adaptation Memo (Prototype → Cooper Use Case)

## Purpose
This project is a **public-data prototype** that demonstrates an end-to-end analytics workflow relevant to Revenue Cycle operations: ingest large healthcare payment/utilization data, apply cleaning + guardrails, derive decision metrics, generate outlier-oriented visuals, and output **review worklists**.  
It is **not** a denial/underpayment system yet because the CMS file lacks payer contract detail, claim IDs, denial codes, and remittance adjustment data.

## What the prototype does today
Using the CMS “Medicare Physician & Other Practitioners — by Provider and Service (2023)” dataset (single-year), the script:
- streams and cleans the dataset from a ZIP
- computes derived metrics:
  - **Impact proxy:** `Allowed_$ ≈ Avg_Mdcr_Alowd_Amt * Tot_Srvcs`
  - **Intensity:** `Tot_Srvcs / Tot_Benes`
  - **Charge compression:** `Avg_Sbmtd_Chrg / Avg_Mdcr_Alowd_Amt`
- produces outlier-oriented visuals:
  - distribution view (heavy tails)
  - concentration by specialty (where dollars sit)
  - charge-compression dispersion by specialty
  - intensity vs compression “triage map”
  - RUCA × specialty heatmap (rural/urban segmentation)
- writes local CSV worklists to support targeted review.

## What this proves (transferable Revenue Cycle skills)
- **Data ingestion at scale:** chunked reads, type coercion, category normalization
- **Data quality & guardrails:** denominator thresholds and ratio handling to reduce false positives
- **Metric engineering:** turning averages + counts into impact proxies and operational ratios
- **Prioritization:** generating ranked “review candidate” lists (worklists)
- **Communication:** visuals designed for triage, not for academic novelty

## Key limitations (why conclusions must be framed as hypotheses)
This CMS file is aggregated at **NPI × HCPCS × Place of Service** and lacks:
- claim IDs / account IDs
- payer plan and contract terms
- denial/reject/remark codes (CARC/RARC)
- adjustment amounts and reasons from remits (835)
- submission/resolution timestamps (timely filing, appeals aging)
- patient responsibility and secondary payer context

Therefore:
- scatter plots that use sampling are for **shape recognition and triage design**, not definitive claims.
- “outliers” are **candidates for review**, not proof of error or wrongdoing.

## How to adapt this into a real Revenue Cycle analytics workflow at Cooper
### Step 1 — Replace/augment the dataset with internal extracts
To make this operational, request extracts that add claim-line detail and denial/remit context.

**Minimum viable internal fields (claim-line level):**
- Claim identifiers: `claim_id`, `account_id`, `line_id`
- Dates: `date_of_service`, `date_billed`, `date_submitted`, `date_paid`, `denial_date`, `appeal_date`, `resolution_date`
- Parties: `rendering_provider`, `billing_provider`, `facility`, `department`
- Payer: `payer`, `plan`, `product`, `network`
- Coding: `HCPCS/CPT`, `modifiers`, `ICD-10`, `units`
- Financials: `billed`, `allowed`, `paid`, `contract_expected` (if available), `patient_resp`, `adjustments_total`
- Denial data: denial category, denial status, denial reason mapping
- Remittance (835): CARC/RARC, adjustment group codes, remark text (where permitted)

### Step 2 — Add a simple data model (so reporting stays stable)
Treat the CMS prototype as a “fact table” practice run, then adopt a clean internal model:

- `fact_claim_line` (one row per claim line)
- `dim_provider`, `dim_department`, `dim_payer`, `dim_service_code`, `dim_time`
- `fact_remit_adjustment` (CARC/RARC adjustments linked to claim lines)

This makes it easy to keep KPI reporting stable even when upstream systems change.

### Step 3 — Convert prototype metrics into Revenue Cycle metrics
The prototype uses proxies; inside Cooper, you upgrade them:

**From** `Allowed_$ ≈ Avg_Allowed * Tot_Srvcs`  
**To** `Allowed_$ = SUM(allowed_amount)` at claim-line level

**From** intensity `Tot_Srvcs / Tot_Benes`  
**To** utilization/throughput measures that matter to Rev Cycle:
- first-pass yield
- denial rate (count + $)
- overturn rate
- underpayment rate
- days to resolution
- timely filing risk queue

### Step 4 — Build the worklists that a Denials/RC analyst actually runs
The prototype’s outlier CSVs become scaffolding for three production worklists.

#### A) Denial Worklist (preventable + high impact)
**Goal:** prioritize denials that are both expensive and fixable.

Core fields:
- denial category (eligibility/auth/medical necessity/coding/COB/timely filing/etc.)
- denial amount (allowed or expected recoverable)
- payer/plan + dept + provider
- days since denial and filing deadlines
- recurrence frequency

Simple scoring (start):
- `Priority = Denied_$ * RecurrenceScore * UrgencyScore`

Better scoring (later):
- `Priority = ExpectedRecoverable_$ * WinProbability * UrgencyFactor`

#### B) Underpayment Worklist (contract variance)
**Goal:** detect “paid < expected” cases and prioritize by dollars and confidence.

Requires:
- contract expected allowed amount (or modeled expected)
- paid amount
- variance = expected - paid

Scoring:
- `Priority = Variance_$ * ConfidenceScore`

#### C) Process Defect Worklist (root cause elimination)
**Goal:** identify where the organization is bleeding repeatedly due to workflow errors.

Examples:
- repeated auth denials in specific departments
- repeated coding denials for specific services/providers
- payer-specific rejections caused by registration errors

Outputs:
- a top-10 list of recurring defect patterns
- recommended owner (registration, coding, clinical dept, contracting)
- before/after KPI tracking

### Step 5 — Use the same guardrails you built in the prototype
The funnel logic in the prototype is not a “nice chart”—it becomes policy:
- don’t flag low-volume noise as operational problems
- tier review thresholds by volume bands
- always show the denominator next to a ratio metric

Practical rules:
- minimum claim-line counts before labeling a provider/service as an outlier
- percentile-based thresholds within peer groups (specialty × department × setting)

### Step 6 — Operationalize: refresh, QA, and feedback loops
To make analytics actionable, add:
- refresh cadence (daily or weekly depending on volume)
- QA checks (missingness spikes, sudden KPI jumps, schema drift)
- an intervention log (what change was made, when, owner)
- tracking: denial rate / cash acceleration / resolution time before vs after

This is the difference between “reporting” and “improving cash flow.”

## How to interpret the current prototype charts (without overclaiming)
### Concentration by specialty
Use it to justify focus:
- where allowed dollars and service volume concentrate, prioritize review effort.

### Charge-to-allowed dispersion by specialty
Use it to identify specialties with:
- unusually high typical compression (median)
- unusually wide spread (dispersion)
These are **candidates** for deeper internal analysis, not conclusions.

### Intensity vs compression “triage map”
Use it to generate candidate review zones:
- high intensity = high operational exposure
- high compression = pricing/charge behavior extremes
Most useful when combined with impact proxy (`Allowed_$`) and denominator filters.

### RUCA × Specialty heatmap
Use it for segmentation:
- rural/urban differences in service intensity by specialty can indicate access and practice-pattern differences.
Inside a hospital system, a similar heatmap by **facility/department** is often even more actionable.

## How to tune the prototype for better “review lists”
The script has levers at the top:
- `MIN_BENES_FOR_OUTLIERS`, `MIN_SERVCS_FOR_OUTLIERS` (noise reduction)
- `TOPK_KEEP` (how many review candidates to export)
- `SAMPLE_FRAC` (visual density; does not change full aggregations)
- Add peer-normalization (recommended next step):
  - compute percentiles or robust z-scores within specialty × place-of-service
  - rank by “unusual relative to peers,” not absolute ratios
